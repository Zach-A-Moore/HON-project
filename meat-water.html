Why Meat Requires So Much Water

Explore the hidden water costs of animal agriculture and meat production.

The Feed Conversion Problem

The primary reason meat consumes so much water is feed conversion inefficiency. Animals don't convert feed directly into meat on a 1:1 basis. Instead, much of the feed is used for the animal's own metabolism, growth, and body maintenance.

Beef
Requires ~25 kg of feed to produce 1 kg of beef. That feed — mostly corn, soy, and hay — all requires water to grow.
Pork
Requires ~6.4 kg of feed to produce 1 kg of pork. Still a significant conversion ratio.
Chicken
Requires ~2.3 kg of feed to produce 1 kg of chicken. The most efficient animal protein in terms of feed conversion.
Direct Comparison
Growing 1 kg of soybeans for direct human consumption uses ~2,000 L of water. Growing the same amount as animal feed multiplies that impact by 25x for beef.
The Water Behind Animal Feed

The majority of meat's water footprint comes not from watering the animal itself, but from growing the massive quantities of feed crops required throughout the animal's lifetime.

For example, a single cow may consume:

  • 7,600 kg of feed over its lifetime
  • 140,000 L of water just to grow that feed (green + blue water)
  • Plus direct water intake for the cow to drink (~200 L per day)
  • Plus water for processing (cleaning, cooling, transport)

Common livestock feed crops and their water footprints:

Soybean
~2,000 L per kg. Often irrigated in water-stressed regions. The majority goes to animal feed globally.
Corn
~1,400 L per kg. Extremely water-intensive, especially in the U.S. Midwest where it's a dominant livestock feed.
Alfalfa
~900 L per kg. A primary hay crop for cattle feed. Often grown in water-scarce regions.
Grain Mix
~1,200 L per kg average. Complex feed blends for swine and poultry contain multiple water-intensive crops.
Regional Water Stress Amplifies the Problem

The water impact of meat is even more severe when considering where the feed is grown. Many major livestock-producing regions rely on irrigation in water-stressed areas.

  • United States (Corn Belt): Heavily irrigated from the Ogallala Aquifer, which is being depleted faster than it recharges.
  • Brazil: Cattle ranching drives deforestation and drains the Pantanal and Amazon watersheds.
  • India: Dairy and beef production compete with drinking water and irrigation for crops.
  • Australia: Cattle feed is grown in drought-prone regions, exacerbating water scarcity during dry years.

When beef is produced in water-stressed regions, the virtual water footprint — the water hidden in the product — represents a real drain on local water resources that may be needed for drinking water or other agricultural crops.

Additional Water in Processing & Transport

Beyond feed and drinking water, significant water is required for:

Slaughter & Processing
Meat processing uses water for cleaning, cooling, sanitation, and product washing. ~5–10 L per kg of meat is typical.
Chilling & Refrigeration
Water is used in cooling towers during storage and transport to maintain meat quality. This can add another 5–15% to the total footprint.
Waste Water
Animal processing facilities generate wastewater containing blood, fat, and manure that requires treatment.
Cleaning & Sanitation
Facilities must maintain strict hygiene standards, using large amounts of water for equipment and floor cleaning.

Real-World Impact: A Single Burger

To visualize the water cost of meat, consider a single hamburger (150g of beef):

Direct Water in Processing
~1.5 L (cleaning, cooling, sanitation)
Feed Crop Water
~3.75 kg of feed × 1,700 L/kg avg = ~6,375 L
Drinking Water & Manure
~500 L (the animal's water needs during the growth period)
Total for One Burger
~6,876 L of water — enough to fill 27 bathtubs or supply drinking water for 1–2 people for a month
Key Takeaways
  • Feed Inefficiency: Beef requires ~25 kg of feed per 1 kg of meat, meaning the water in feed far exceeds the water directly used on the animal.
  • Crop Water Dominates: 95%+ of meat's water footprint comes from growing feed crops, not from watering the animal itself.
  • Regional Impact: Meat produced in water-stressed regions (U.S., Middle East, India, Australia) draws from overexploited aquifers and rivers.
  • Efficiency Varies: Chicken and fish are more water-efficient than beef and pork, but all animal products exceed plant-based alternatives.
  • Reduction Works: Eating less meat — even one meatless day per week — can save thousands of liters of water annually.
